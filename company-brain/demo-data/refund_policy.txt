## Overview

This document is the internal source of truth for all refund decisions at Loopline. The customer-facing version lives at loopline.com/policies/refunds — keep them in sync at the next quarterly review. This doc has more detail and explicit edge cases.

If you're a CS rep reading this for the first time: the short version is in the Quick Reference at the bottom. Read that first, then come back.

## The 30-day window

Customers can request a refund within 30 days of their first paid charge. The clock starts on first successful charge — not signup, not trial start. After 30 days, refunds become discretionary (see "Partial refunds" below).

Eligibility checks before processing any refund:
1. Has the customer been charged at least once? (Trial-only accounts → no refund needed, just cancel the trial.)
2. Is the charge less than 30 days old? Use Stripe's `created` timestamp on the Charge object, not our `subscriptions.created_at`. They sometimes differ by minutes.
3. Has this customer received a refund on a prior subscription? If yes, escalate to Alex Morgan before processing — repeat refunds are a fraud signal.

## Always-approved categories

These refunds are processed without escalation, regardless of amount or window:

1. **Enterprise / VIP customers** (any tier, any amount) — refund any amount, any reason, within reason. Don't argue, don't ask for documentation. Loop in their AE in #cs-enterprise so the account team isn't surprised.
2. **Defective product** — if the customer can demonstrate the product didn't work as advertised (specific feature broken, repeated downtime, data loss): full refund, no questions, no required documentation. We eat that one. Be generous in interpretation. If a customer says "X was supposed to work and didn't, I want my money back", we believe them unless server logs clearly contradict.
3. **Duplicate charges, billing errors, our fault on switch/cancel** — full refund of the erroneous amount. Always.

## Partial refunds (mid-cycle cancellations)

When a customer cancels mid-billing-cycle and asks for a refund of the unused portion:

- **Monthly plans**: pro-rated refund based on days unused. Calculation: `(days_unused / days_in_period) * monthly_charge`. Round to 2 decimals. Process via Stripe `refunds.create` against the most recent charge.
- **Annual plans**: NO automatic pro-rated refund — annual plans are sold at a discount precisely because the customer commits. We can offer a Stripe credit toward future use within 12 months, but not a cash refund. Exception: if the customer is leaving because of a defect we caused (see "Defective product" above), full pro-rated cash refund.

Day-1 sanity check before processing: Stripe occasionally double-charges if a webhook retried. Look for two charges within 60 seconds of each other on the same customer — if you see that, refund the duplicate first, then evaluate the partial-cycle refund on the remaining real charge.

## Stripe credit vs actual refund — choose correctly

Two ways to give money back. Picking wrong wastes processor fees or leaves the customer waiting for ACH for a week.

**Stripe credit (preferred when applicable)**: applied to the customer's next invoice via `customerBalance.create`. Use this when:
- Customer is staying on the platform.
- Refund is for goodwill or partial period adjustment.
- Customer is on a recurring plan.
- Refund is less than 60 days old AND the customer wants to remain a customer.

Stripe credit costs us less (no payment processor fee, no chargeback exposure) and keeps the relationship going.

**Actual refund (Stripe `refunds.create`)**: money goes back to the original payment method. Use this when:
- Customer is leaving the platform (or already left).
- Refund is for defective product or our error.
- Customer explicitly asks for cash back, not credit. (Don't argue — give them what they ask for.)
- Refund is more than 60 days old. Stripe blocks credits past that window.

For actual refunds: Stripe takes 5–10 business days to settle for cards, longer for ACH and SEPA. Tell the customer this proactively so they don't follow up on day 3.

## Edge cases

**Customer claims they never authorized the charge.** Treat as potentially fraudulent. Pull Stripe logs (IP, user-agent, 3DS status). If clearly the customer's session: process the refund and document. If unclear: escalate to Alex + Priyanka before refunding. Do NOT refund a possible chargeback proactively — we lose the dispute leverage.

**Chargeback already filed by the customer.** Don't refund — let the chargeback process play out. Refunding on top of a chargeback double-pays. Reach out to the customer asking them to drop the chargeback in exchange for a faster direct refund. If they won't drop it, fight it through Stripe Dispute with the evidence pack.

**Customer is angry and threatening to post on Twitter / X / file BBB complaint.** Don't let the threat dictate the refund decision — apply the same policy. But escalate to Alex so we have visibility on potential PR exposure. Maya wants a heads-up on anything that could hit social.

**Customer used >50 GB of data, then asks for refund.** Heavy usage is signal that the product worked. Decline the "defective" pathway. Offer pro-rated mid-cycle refund per partial-refund rules above.

**Annual customer 6 months in, asking for refund because they're switching to a competitor.** Decline cash refund per annual plan rules. Offer a Stripe credit equal to remaining months that they can apply toward future Loopline use within 12 months. Document reason in HubSpot — competitor-loss is product feedback we want.

**Customer charged the wrong card / wants the refund routed to a different card.** Stripe doesn't allow it. Refund to the original method only. If their original card is canceled, Stripe will issue a check/ACH per their fallback. Tell the customer this is Stripe's rule, not ours.

**Refund crosses fiscal-year boundary.** No special handling on our side, but flag Priyanka in #finance — accounting wants the heads-up.

## Logging

Every refund must be logged in #refund-log within 1 hour of processing. Format:

    Customer: <workspace name>
    Plan: <starter | team | growth | enterprise>
    Amount: $<amount>
    Reason: <one of: defective | enterprise-courtesy | mid-cycle | duplicate-charge | goodwill | other>
    Method: <stripe-credit | stripe-refund>
    Approved by: <self | alex | alex+priyanka | marcus | maya>
    Stripe ID: <re_... or cr_...>
    Notes: <one line if anything's unusual>

We review #refund-log monthly to spot patterns (e.g. recurring "defective" reasons pointing at a real bug, or a single rep approving many borderline cases).
